Abstract

This study investigates the automatic detection of travel modes using GPS-based movement data and Google Timeline data. The goal is to evaluate how the methodological approach of travel mode detection with parameters like speed and acceleration as well as road type perform in classifying transportation modes such as walking, cycling, car, and train. A key focus is the enhancement of detection accuracy through the integration of OpenStreetMap road type information as contextual features. Additionally, the study compares how data quality and structure from different tracking methods influence classification performance and how google maps classifying system performs in comparison.

Introduction

Research Questions

  • Is travel mode detection using GPS data combined with overlaid road type information from OpenStreetMap and parameters as speed feasible and accurate?
  • How well can travel modes be detected using different tracking methods (Google Timeline vs. GPS Logger)?
  • How do the two data sources differ in terms of accuracy and reliability for travel mode classification?

Results / products

The results will be evaluated based on the number of correctly identified travel modes. The analysis will focus on: * Which travel modes (e.g., walking, cycling, car, train) can be detected most reliably * Which tracking method (Google Timeline vs. GPS Logger) produces higher classification accuracy Another result can be a comparison of travel modes of the two individuals.

It is expected that transport modes with distinct speed patterns, such as walking and train travel, can be detected more reliably than modes with overlapping characteristics, such as cycling and slow motorized traffic. Furthermore, GPS Logger data is anticipated to achieve higher classification accuracy due to its higher temporal resolution. However, the results strongly depend on data quality, particularly GPS gaps, positional accuracy, and the irregular sampling intervals of Google Timeline data. In addition, road type information may significantly improve the classification by providing additional spatial context.

Data

This project uses two primary mobility data sources, tracked from two individuals: Google Timeline data and GPS Logger data. Google Timeline provides sparse but semantically enriched location records, including latitude/longitude coordinates, timestamps, and partially pre-classified activity labels (e.g., walking, driving, cycling). However, its temporal resolution is low and irregular, which limits fine-grained movement analysis.

In contrast, GPS Logger data offers high-frequency, continuous tracking at fixed time intervals, containing raw coordinates and timestamps. This allows for detailed trajectory reconstruction but introduces noise, tracking holes (crashing app) and requires preprocessing.

To enhance both datasets, contextual geographic information from OpenStreetMap is incorporated, particularly road type classifications (e.g., motorway, residential, cycleway). This external context data is retrieved via OpenStreetMap APIs and spatial joins.

Analytical concepts

This project focuses on the analysis and classification of movement trajectories to detect different travel modes from GPS tracking data. The analytical workflow includes preprocessing and segmentation of trajectories into meaningful movement segments, followed by the extraction of movement features such as speed, acceleration, distance, and temporal patterns. The analysis will primarily rely on rule-based speed categorization to distinguish between different movement types. Thresholds based on average and maximum speeds will be used to classify trajectories into categories such as walking, cycling, train and motorized transport. In addition, spatial overlay techniques will be applied to integrate contextual information from road network and road type data, allowing movement segments to be linked with infrastructure characteristics such as highways, residential roads, or pedestrian paths. The individuals Knowledge of their movements will validate the accuracy of the categorization.

R concepts

The project will mainly be implemented in R using packages from the tidyverse ecosystem for data wrangling and preprocessing. Spatial data processing and trajectory analysis will be performed using packages such as sf, lubridate, and osmdata. For visualization, tools such as ggplot2, leaflet, or tmap may be used to create maps and exploratory plots.The package moveHMM may be used to experiment with Hidden Markov Models for travel mode detection. Additional concepts include feature engineering, trajectory segmentation, spatial joins, and overlay analysis with OpenStreetMap road network data.

Risk analysis

One of the main challenges of this project is the quality and consistency of the collected data. GPS noise and inaccuracy, missing points, irregular sampling intervals (especially in Google Timeline data), and tracking gaps may reduce the accuracy of travel mode detection. So the risk of limited amount of self-collected movement data can result in less robustness or generalization and some transport modes may be underrepresented. In addition, some transport modes may overlap in their speed ranges (for example slow cycling and running or walking and waiting phases), making rule-based classification difficult.

As a contingency plan, simpler and more robust approaches such as rule-based speed classification combined with road type information can be used instead of more complex models. Publicly available datasets such as the GeoLife dataset may additionally be integrated to increase the amount of available data. Another plan can be to experment with the package moveHMM which is able to detect travel modes, but could be complex to implement.
